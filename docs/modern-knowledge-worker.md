# Modern Knowledge Worker in the Age of AI

> The unit of leverage is no longer raw information — it's the ability to structure, retrieve, and act on knowledge faster than the problem evolves.

## What Has Changed

AI models can now draft, summarize, translate, and reason across large bodies of text in seconds. This does not eliminate the knowledge worker — it raises the bar for what they must contribute. Tasks that were once valuable (information gathering, first-draft writing, basic synthesis) are increasingly automated. What remains irreplaceable is judgment, context, and the ability to build systems that keep knowledge alive and usable.

The modern knowledge worker is less a producer of documents and more a **curator and architect of knowledge flows**.

---

## Core Skills

### 1. Prompt Engineering and AI Collaboration
- Write clear, structured prompts that constrain AI output to useful formats
- Iterate with AI rather than accepting first outputs — treat it as a fast but literal junior colleague
- Know when to delegate to AI and when human judgment is non-negotiable
- Recognize hallucinations and model limitations; verify AI output against primary sources

### 2. Structured Thinking and Knowledge Decomposition
- Break complex problems into clearly scoped questions AI can help answer
- Separate facts from inferences from opinions in any document you create
- Write for machines as well as humans — use consistent terminology, headings, and metadata
- Think in systems: how does this piece of knowledge connect to and update other pieces?

### 3. Knowledge Architecture
- Design folder structures and tagging schemes that age well
- Understand the difference between reference material (stable, fact-checked) and working notes (ephemeral, speculative)
- Write documents that are self-contained enough to be understood without the conversation that created them
- Link aggressively — isolated notes rot; linked notes compound

### 4. Critical Evaluation of AI Output
- Fact-check claims, especially statistics, dates, and named sources
- Understand that AI models have training cutoffs and may reflect outdated consensus
- Use AI summaries as a starting point, not a conclusion
- Maintain epistemic standards: know your sources

### 5. Async and Written Communication
- Default to writing over meetings — written decisions are searchable, AI-readable, and auditable
- Write decision records: what was decided, why, what was rejected and why
- Keep context close to the artifact (not in someone's head or a chat thread)

### 6. Workflow and Automation Literacy
- Connect tools with APIs and automation layers (Zapier, Make, n8n, custom scripts)
- Understand what can be triggered, scheduled, or delegated to an AI agent
- Audit and refine automated pipelines — automation drifts silently

## Own Your Knowledge Base

This is the most important systemic habit a knowledge worker can build.

### Why it matters
A knowledge base you own — in plain files, under your control — is the infrastructure layer for everything else you do. It is readable by AI agents today and by tools that don't exist yet. It does not lock you into a vendor. It compounds: every note you write makes the next one easier to find, connect, and act on.

### What it enables
- **Other people** can onboard faster because context is written down, not in your head
- **AI agents** (like Claude Code, AutoGPT, or custom pipelines) can read your `docs/` folder directly and answer questions, generate reports, or take action on your behalf
- **Your future self** can recover context after weeks away from a topic
- **Teams** can build shared understanding without synchronous meetings

### How to maintain it
- Write the minimum viable note: one clear heading, key facts, a link or two
- Update in place — don't create a new file every time something changes
- Review quarterly: archive what's stale, promote drafts that have stabilized
- Keep it boring: flat structure beats clever hierarchy every time

### What belongs here
- Decisions and their rationale
- Processes and how-tos
- Reference material you return to repeatedly
- Meeting outcomes (not transcripts — outcomes)
- Research notes with source links
- Templates

### What does not belong here
- First drafts and raw brainstorms (keep those in a scratchpad)
- Chat logs or email threads (extract the insight, discard the noise)
- Duplicated content from primary sources (link instead)

## A Practical Mindset Shift

| Old model | New model |
|-----------|-----------|
| Write documents for humans to read | Write documents for humans **and** AI agents to read |
| Knowledge lives in your head | Knowledge lives in a file your team and tools can query |
| Meetings transfer context | Documents transfer context; meetings make decisions |
| Search for answers | Your knowledge base surfaces answers before you ask |
| Produce information | Curate and structure information so others can act on it |

- [ ] Pick one topic you know well — write a one-page reference doc on it
- [ ] Link it to at least two related notes

The knowledge worker who builds this habit early will operate at a different level of leverage than one who does not.
